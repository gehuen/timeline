Salt March Timeline

Gerard

March 2, 1930

1

Mahatma Gandhi sends a letter to the Viceroy of India, Lord Irwin, stating his 11 demands and his intent to start the march if they're not met.

March 12, 1930

2

Gandhi sets out on foot from his ashram, with a small group of followers to march to the town of Dandi.

Significance

This is the start of the salt march and a key part to India's truggle for independence. The salt march would start a chain event and propel India's goal of freedom from Britian. The salt march got more people to know about India's struggle for freedom aswell as it motivated more Indians to fight for their country.

March 18, 1930

3

Gandhi and his followers cross the Sabarmati river and continue their march towards Dandi. The march attracts more people along the way, and the group size increases.

April 6, 1930

4

Gandhi and his followers reach the village of Dandi and break the British salt laws by collecting salt from the beach.

Significance

Breaking the salt law showed that the Indian people were willing to get arrrested to fight for their rights. This was a powerful symbol and would motivate other Indians to fight for their rights. The act of challenging Britian's salt monopoly symbolized strength and determination to rebel. The action of non-violent protest showed the Indians seeked change to India without resorting to violence.

May 6, 1930

5

The British authorities arrest Gandhi, along with thousands of other Indian nationalists who have joined the salt march.

Significance

Ghandi's arrest brought international attention to India's fight for freedom. With more eyes on India, the British became nervous of the growing Indian forces. Ghandi was a prominent figure for India, so his arrest further motivated the people of India. The arrest also showed Britian's unwillingness to accept the Indians demands for freedom.

July 5, 1930

6

The first Round Table Conference begins in London, where Indian leaders discuss reforms with British officials.

January 26, 1931

7

The Indian National Congress declares Independence Day, and the Indian flag is hoisted on public buildings across the country.

March 5, 1931

8

Gandhi and Lord Irwin sign the Gandhi-Irwin Pact, which allows the civil disobedience campaign to end and releases political prisoners.

Significance

The signing of this pact showed that the efforts the Indian had a result, as this signified that the British government was willing to negotiate with Indians. This pact marked the Indian National congress as a legitimate representative of the Indian people. This would provide India more negotiating power and would be a crucial step to India becoming free from Britian.

September 8, 1932

9

The British government announces the Communal Award, which granted the lowest class in India to vote for their own government representative.

Significance

This would cause further divide between the people of India and went against Ghandi's ideology of a united India. He would protest this through fasting. Despite this award giving a voice to the lower classes of India, it was a change that was only positive. This opportunity caused divide between the castes. Despite the mixed reactions to this award, it showed recognition to lower classes and the importance and equality in a political stage.

January 30, 1948

10

Ghandi is assassinated at age 78 by Hindu extremist.
